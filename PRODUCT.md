# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Software engineers, senior developers, hiring managers, and technical recruiters who arrive from a job application, GitHub profile, or direct referral. They scan in under 20 seconds to decide whether the person behind the portfolio is worth a deeper look. They're evaluating for technical depth and signal-to-noise ratio, not visual flair.

## Product Purpose

Personal portfolio for David Alves, a software engineer. The site exists to establish credibility and make it immediately obvious that serious, precise engineering work lives here. Success means a visitor closes the tab having formed the impression: this person ships real things and cares about craft.

## Brand Personality

Confident, precise, direct. The site says what it means and nothing more. No soft-sell, no hype, no filler text that restates the heading. The design itself is the argument.

## Anti-references

- Generic SaaS cream: off-white backgrounds, blue/purple gradient accents, hero metrics, identical card grids. Feels like a template, not a person.
- Portfolio maximalism: particle backgrounds, animated counters, hover effects on every element. Decoration masking thin content.
- The "here are my skills as progress bars" style: no competence radars, technology icon walls, or percentage meters.

## Design Principles

1. The work is the proof. Let projects and blog carry the credibility; don't pad with superlatives or skill meters.
2. Engineering confidence, not engineering cosplay. Structural discipline and precise spacing signal craft; forced darkness or terminal aesthetics are costume.
3. Every element earns its place. If removing it doesn't cost anything, remove it.
4. Friction-free scanning. A visitor in 15 seconds should reach the same conclusion as one in 3 minutes.
5. Dark by scene, not by default. The audience works at desks and in dim rooms; a controlled dark palette fits the context without performing it.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA. Good contrast on all text, keyboard-navigable components, semantic HTML throughout. No reduced-motion assumption; respect `prefers-reduced-motion` for any transitions.
